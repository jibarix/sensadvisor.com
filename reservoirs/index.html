Puerto Rico Reservoir Levels

An independent comparison of measured reservoir levels with PRASA's published operating thresholds.
What this page shows

Measured water levels at Puerto Rico's principal reservoirs, from USGS sensors, placed against PRASA's official operating thresholds so you can see where each reservoir sits relative to those published lines. Where the model can, it adds an experimental 30-day projection.

It is not an official water-supply forecast, not a rationing announcement, and the level percentages are position within the operating range — not percent of a reservoir's total water capacity.

30-day outlook — experimental projection SENS model · modeled reservoirs · not an official forecast

SENS estimate An experimental water-balance model for four of the nineteen embalses — not an official forecast.

In plain language: these charts show three possible paths — not predictions — for the next 30 days, under historically dry, typical, and wet conditions. They cover only the four reservoirs whose USGS gauges let the model close a water balance.

How this model works

The model runs for four reservoirs — Patillas plus Carraízo, La Plata, and Cerrillos — because a water balance only closes where USGS gauges measure both the inflow (covering at least 60% of the dam's drainage) and the below-dam releases. The other fifteen embalses on the status board carry the elevation gauge but not those discharge gauges, so their withdrawal can't be bounded — they still get the level-and-band read above.

Storage is marched 30 days ahead under three inflow scenarios drawn from 36 years of calendar climatology — dry, typical, and wet — a range of where the level could go. The model result below each reservoir reads off the conservative dry path: roughly how many days a continued-dry spell would take to carry the level down to each PRASA line (the dashed lines drawn here and on the history chart). The dry arm also stresses withdrawal to drought-year demand, so it is deliberately conservative and assumes no emergency releases. Separately, a near-term rain tilt reads the live NWS 7-day rain forecast against each site's historical 7-day rainfall and leans the drier or wetter arm — the dry/typical/wet score bar on each card, uncalibrated, and it does not move the dry-path result.

Methodology & validation how the utilization and outlook layers are built

1. Level to storage

USGS damsite elevation is converted to stored volume with the latest survey-specific stage-capacity curve. Local lake area is derived from the curve slope: Alake = dV/dh.

2. Daily water balance

ΔS = I + P·Alake − E·Alake − O − R − W

W is the residual left after inflow, rain on the lake, evaporation, spill, and controlled releases. It is not measured directly — it also absorbs seepage, ungauged inflow, and rating/sensor error, so it is an upper-bounded estimate of withdrawal, not a clean canal meter reading. Daily discharge inputs come from USGS series where available; rainfall and reference-ET inputs come from GOES-PRWEB grids.

3. Reporting cadence

The balance is computed daily. Daily and sub-annual values are diagnostic; annual or rolling-annual values are reportable because short windows are more affected by release timing, missing inputs, and measurement noise.

4. Scenario paths + near-term tilt

The 30-day dry/typical/wet paths use 36 years of calendar-day inflow climatology, a 5 mm/day evaporation baseline, lake-rain scenarios, trailing release, and bounded annual demand. The three paths are a hypothetical range, not a weather forecast and not ranked by likelihood; the dry floor assumes no emergency release. Separately, a near-term precipitation tilt scores the live NWS api.weather.gov 7-day QPF at each reservoir's anchor point against that point's NOAA GHCN-Daily 7-day rainfall history (1990–2025) and expresses the wetness percentile as uncalibrated scenario scores (a near-term rain tilt) over the three arms. It leans toward whichever arm the coming week's rain favors — it does not move the conservative dry-path runway — and the scores are an uncalibrated heuristic, not probabilities, until a QPF reforecast backtest exists.

Validation: Patillas is the direct calibration anchor. The metered canal sits inside the 95% uncertainty band in both full-year windows: 2020, 12.1 versus 12.0 MGD (+1%); and 2021, 13.1 versus 12.5 MGD (+5%). A strict screen across all nineteen USGS-gauged embalses — requiring a live below-dam release gauge plus inflow gauges covering at least 60% of the dam's drainage — passes three: Carraizo, La Plata, and Cerrillos. The other fifteen lack one of those gauges (most have no active below-dam release; Guajataca and Cidra have the release gauge but almost no inflow gauge). Cross-checks include mass-balance closure, interannual behavior, drought response, and plant-capacity magnitude. These checks support bounded estimates but are not independent canal ground truth for every reservoir.
Limits: Results are sensitive to curve age, ungauged inflow, operational releases, throughflow, rainfall/evaporation estimates, and gap-filled inputs. Treat utilization values as bounded analytical estimates and the outlook as decision support—not an official PRASA rationing notice.
