Each bar is normalized to a reservoir's original design storage (100%): the solid blue part is the share still usable as of the last bathymetric study, the faded brown part has silted in. 30-day outlook modeled reservoirs · scenario cone + continued-dry runway

A forward water-balance projection for the four reservoirs covered by the bounded utilization model. Storage is marched 30 days ahead under three inflow scenarios drawn from 36 years of calendar climatology — dry, normal, and wet — producing a fan of where the level could go. The runway below each is the number of days a continued-dry spell would take to carry the reservoir down to each PRASA alert line (the same dashed lines drawn here and on the history chart). The dry arm also stresses withdrawal to the reservoir's drought-year demand, so the runway is deliberately conservative. The cone itself is a climatology spread, not a weather forecast; layered on top, a near-term tilt reads the live NWS 7-day rain forecast against each site's rainfall normal and leans the odds toward the drier or wetter arm — a probability hint for the coming week (shown as the dry/normal/wet bar on each card), not a calibrated 30-day forecast. The dry floor assumes no emergency releases.

Methodology & validation how the utilization and outlook layers are built

1. Level to storage

USGS damsite elevation is converted to stored volume with the latest survey-specific stage-capacity curve. Local lake area is derived from the curve slope: Alake = dV/dh.

2. Daily water balance

ΔS = I + P·Alake − E·Alake − O − R − W

W is the residual left after inflow, rain on the lake, evaporation, spill, and controlled releases. It is not measured directly — it also absorbs seepage, ungauged inflow, and rating/sensor error, so it is an upper-bounded estimate of withdrawal, not a clean canal meter reading. Daily discharge inputs come from USGS series where available; rainfall and reference-ET inputs come from GOES-PRWEB grids.

3. Reporting cadence

The balance is computed daily. Daily and sub-annual values are diagnostic; annual or rolling-annual values are reportable because short windows are more affected by release timing, missing inputs, and measurement noise.

4. Forecast cone + near-term tilt

The 30-day dry/normal/wet paths use 36 years of calendar-day inflow climatology, a 5 mm/day evaporation baseline, lake-rain scenarios, trailing release, and bounded annual demand. The spread is a scenario range, not a weather forecast; the dry floor assumes no emergency release. Separately, a near-term precipitation tilt scores the live NWS api.weather.gov 7-day QPF at each reservoir's anchor point against that point's NOAA GHCN-Daily rainfall normal (1990–2025) and expresses the wetness percentile as a probability lean over the three scenarios. It re-weights which arm is likelier for the coming week — it does not move the conservative dry-path runway — and the weights are an uncalibrated heuristic until a QPF reforecast backtest exists.

Validation: Patillas is the direct calibration anchor. The metered canal sits inside the 95% uncertainty band in both full-year windows: 2020, 12.1 versus 12.0 MGD (+1%); and 2021, 13.1 versus 12.5 MGD (+5%). The production set is Carraizo, La Plata, and Cerrillos; Guayabal and Guajataca were screened out. Cross-checks include mass-balance closure, interannual behavior, drought response, and plant-capacity magnitude. These checks support bounded estimates but are not independent canal ground truth for every reservoir.
Limits: Results are sensitive to curve age, ungauged inflow, operational releases, throughflow, rainfall/evaporation estimates, and gap-filled inputs. Treat utilization values as bounded analytical estimates and the outlook as decision support—not an official PRASA rationing notice.
